Consulting Exercise · Business Systems Analyst

Triage under pressure, an integration failure, and a full donor-payments program redesign

A nonprofit organization brought me in for a consulting case exercise: three realistic operational scenarios, presented and defended live under questioning, no scripted answer, just the working method I'd actually use. This page presents the content of that engagement; the organization's name has been withheld.

Format · 10-slide presentation, 60-75 min interactive session Scenarios · 3, spanning prioritization, incident response, and process redesign Method · Live presentation and defense, no pre-shared questions
Scenario A

Managing competing priorities

Triage by impact first, then communicate the trade-offs

The scenario: a system implementation, a backlog of user support tickets, and a data cleanup effort are all flagged urgent at once, with no formal triage criteria in place. The question underneath it is whether "urgent" and "consequential" are actually the same thing.

Ring-fence first

System implementation

Vendor dependencies and contract milestones create compounding risk if delayed. Protect the critical path.

Triage daily

User support tickets

Blocking issues handled same-day with a defined escalation path. Non-blocking issues batched into a queue.

Schedule it

Data cleanup

No go-live dependency and can absorb a short delay, provided the timeline is communicated clearly.

"Not everything called urgent is equally consequential. I ask what breaks, and for how long, if this slips one week, and that question cuts through the noise."
Scenario B

Integration failure: a 3-day sync gap

Two parallel tracks, unblock the business first, diagnose in parallel

The donation platform hasn't synced to the CRM in three days, and downstream communications that depend on that data need to go out today. Splitting the response into two simultaneous tracks keeps the business moving instead of waiting on a root cause.

Track 1 · Unblock now

  • Scope the impact in the first 30 minutes: is data lost or just delayed? That distinction changes the severity and the response.
  • Pull a manual export from the donation platform, validate it, and deploy today's communications from it.
  • Business doesn't stop. Operations continue while root cause work runs in parallel, not sequentially after it.

Track 2 · Root cause, in parallel

  • Pull error logs and find the exact timestamp the sync stopped. What changed in the 24 hours prior?
  • Check the common culprits first: API key expiry, a schema change, a rate limit, or a failed deployment.
  • Connect the technical symptom to the business timeline directly, rather than just handing findings off.

Stakeholder communication

  • Status note within hours covering what's known, the workaround, and an ETA.
  • Updates every 4-6 hours, with a close-out note that documents the root cause.

Preventing recurrence

  • Integration health alerting on a volume threshold, so a silent failure gets caught in hours, not days.
  • A runbook for this failure mode, plus a post-incident review within 48 hours.
"Three days of silence is a monitoring gap, not just a technical failure."
Scenario C

Redesigning the monthly giving program

Elicit before designing, quantify before building, name an owner before go-live

Monthly donor payments run across multiple systems with variable charge dates and limited follow-up on failures. Responsibility is split across fundraising, finance, and operations with no single end-to-end owner. This was the largest scenario, worked in four parts.

01

Understand current state

Structured interviews across all three teams in parallel, not sequentially, plus job shadowing, document analysis of 12-24 months of transaction data before any interview, and a joint workshop to align findings. Output: As-Is and To-Be BPMN process maps.

02

Quantify the gaps

Four structural gaps: split payment systems, variable charge dates, no formal recovery process, and fragmented ownership. Modeled as a hypothesis, not a claim, using stated assumptions the team would validate.

03

Design the future state

Standardized charge dates chosen at sign-up, a formal dunning sequence as a defined business rule set, and an explicit RACI across fundraising, finance, and IT/operations, signed off before anything gets built.

04

Drive adoption

A phased 5-6 month rollout, success metrics defined before build starts, and a pilot on new donors only, with the existing base untouched until the design is validated.

Closing the Session

Questions for the panel

I closed by asking the panel three questions of my own, to check my approach against how the organization actually operates rather than how the scenarios assumed it did: whether the three teams already meet regularly or operate independently, whether there'd be a dedicated operational owner post-implementation, and how much appetite there was for process change versus a system replacement.

Conclusion

Three principles across all three scenarios

A

Structure before solutions

Triage by impact, not urgency. Elicitation before design. Root cause before fixing.

B

Ownership is non-negotiable

A RACI signed off before build starts. A named owner in place at go-live, not assumed.

C

Outcomes, not just outputs

Measurable outcomes defined before build starts. Success tracked against an agreed baseline. Adoption monitored, not assumed.

Why this is on the portfolio
